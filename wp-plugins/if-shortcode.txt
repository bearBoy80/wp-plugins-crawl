The syntax is the following:<\/p>\n<pre><code>[if condition1 condition2=false condition3]{content}[\/if]\n<\/code><\/pre>\n<p>Conditions are passed as attribute names. Multiple conditions evaluate to the result of ORing all of them. In other words,<br \/>\nif at least one condition evaluates to the desired boolean result, {content} is rendered, otherwise it is discarded.<br \/>\nAttribute values determine if we want the condition to be true or false. A value of &#8216;0&#8217;, &#8216;false&#8217;, &#8221; (the empty string),<br \/>\n&#8216;null&#8217; or &#8216;no&#8217; means we expect the condition to be false. When used<br \/>\noutside an [if] block, these shortcodes behave as if the whole content is surrounded by an [if] shortcode whose condition<br \/>\nevaluates to true. In other words, an [else] shortcode would not render any content, while a [eitherway] one would. You can<br \/>\nuse as many of these shortcodes as you like in a single [if] block, which gives you the ability to do things like:<\/p>\n<pre><code>    - Am I logged in?\n    [if is_user_logged_in]- Yes you are.\n    [else]- No you are not.\n    [\/else][eitherway]- I'm sorry, what?\n    [\/eitherway]- I said YOU A-R-E LOGGED IN!!!\n    [else]- YOU ARE NOT LOGGED IN!!!
