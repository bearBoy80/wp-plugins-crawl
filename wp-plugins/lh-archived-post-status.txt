\/wp:paragraph -->\n\n<!-- wp:paragraph -->\n<p>But that's not the case. A <em>real<\/em> Archive is a place where you can store posts that are not relevant any longer \u2014 thus keeping them away from the Loop \u2014 <em>but without deleting them<\/em>, because, naturally enough, the whole point of <em>having<\/em> an Archive is to allow search engines and backlinks to 'old' articles to still work correctly.<\/p>\n<!-- \/wp:paragraph -->\n\n<!-- wp:paragraph -->\n<p>This is the whole point of this simple plugin: to allow to add a <em>new<\/em> status to every post\/page, classifying it as having been archived. Instead, posts and pages stay exactly as they were before \u2014 just with a <em>new<\/em> status (beyond the classic ones, i.e., published, private, draft).<\/p>\n<!-- \/wp:paragraph -->\n\n<!-- wp:paragraph -->\n<p>Because this status is an <em>addition<\/em>, this means that all well-behaved plugins and themes will <em>not<\/em> recognise it as a <em>valid<\/em> status (i.e. when checking if a post is published, the theme\/plugin will <em>not<\/em> find the archived posts), and so they effectively 'disappear' from the visibility status of most 3rd party code. That's exactly what we expect to happen with an 'archived' post. And that's precisely what this plugin does, and does quite well; as such, it comes as highly recommended by many reviewers, and ranks high on their lists of 'best' plugins for a specific purpose.<\/p>\n<!-- \/wp:paragraph -->\n\n<!-- wp:paragraph -->\n<p>The plugin itself is well-written, strictly follows all coding guidelines as set by Automattic and the developer community, regularly maintained, and has very reasonable support on the forums: issues are opened, followed up by the author, and closed when resolved or when threads become stale due to lack of activity.
